# Manchester United: The complete trophy cabinet

Manchester United have won **68 trophies** across all recognised competitions, making them the most decorated club in English football history. Their haul includes **20 league titles**, **13 FA Cups**, **6 League Cups**, **3 European Cups/Champions Leagues**, **1 Europa League**, **1 Cup Winners' Cup**, **1 UEFA Super Cup**, **1 Intercontinental Cup**, **1 FIFA Club World Cup**, and **21 Community Shield/Charity Shield titles**. The conventional "major trophy" count — excluding the Community Shield — stands at **47**. The bulk of this silverware arrived under two legendary managers: Sir Matt Busby (who rebuilt the club twice) and Sir Alex Ferguson (who delivered **38 trophies** between 1986 and 2013). Data below is verified through the 2023–24 season and cross-referenced across the official Manchester United website, UEFA.com, Wikipedia, ESPN, and specialist databases.

The **20 league championships** are tied for the all-time English record (level with Liverpool as of 2024–25). Seven came in the pre-Premier League era; all 13 Premier League titles were won under Sir Alex Ferguson.

A notable **26-year gap** separates the last First Division title (1966–67) from the inaugural Premier League title (1992–93). The early titles were won under Ernest Mangnall (1908, 1911); the mid-century run belongs entirely to Sir Matt Busby (1952–1967).

## 2. European Cup / UEFA Champions League: 3

Three European Cup/Champions League triumphs span four decades, each carrying immense historical weight. The 1968 win made United the **first English club** to lift the trophy; the 1999 win completed an unprecedented Treble; the 2008 win was decided by a dramatic penalty shootout.

### Results

| # | Season | Opponent | Result | Venue |
|---|--------|----------|--------|-------|
| 1 | 1967–68 | Benfica | **4–1** (a.e.t.) | Wembley Stadium, London |
| 2 | 1998–99 | Bayern Munich | **2–1** | Camp Nou, Barcelona |
| 3 | 2007–08 | Chelsea | **1–1** (a.e.t.), **6–5 pens** | Luzhniki Stadium, Moscow |

### Key details

**1967–68 vs Benfica (4–1 a.e.t.):** The match stood at 1–1 after 90 minutes (Bobby Charlton 53'; Jaime Graça 79'). In extra time, George Best (92'), Brian Kidd (94') and Charlton again (99') sealed the victory. Managed by Sir Matt Busby, this triumph came ten years after the Munich air disaster, with survivors Charlton and Bill Foulkes in the starting lineup.

**1998–99 vs Bayern Munich (2–1):** Bayern led through Mario Basler's 6th-minute free kick for almost the entire match. In the most famous comeback in Champions League history, substitutes **Teddy Sheringham (91') and Ole Gunnar Solskjær (93')** scored in injury time. This completed the **Treble** — Premier League, FA Cup, and Champions League in a single season — under Sir Alex Ferguson.

**2007–08 vs Chelsea (1–1 a.e.t., 6–5 on penalties):** The first all-English Champions League final. Cristiano Ronaldo (26') headed United ahead; Frank Lampard (45') equalised. In the shootout, John Terry slipped and hit the post when a conversion would have won it for Chelsea; **Edwin van der Sar** then saved Nicolas Anelka's kick to clinch the trophy. Ronaldo had his penalty saved during the shootout but United still prevailed.

## 3. FA Cup: 13

Manchester United's **13 FA Cups** place them second on the all-time list behind Arsenal (14). The span runs from 1909 to 2024, with finals occasionally requiring replays or going to extra time — though none of United's wins were ever decided by penalties.

### Results

| # | Season | Opponent | Result | Notes |
|---|--------|----------|--------|-------|
| 1 | 1908–09 | Bristol City | **1–0** | Crystal Palace ground, London |
| 2 | 1947–48 | Blackpool | **4–2** | Wembley |
| 3 | 1962–63 | Leicester City | **3–1** | Wembley |
| 4 | 1976–77 | Liverpool | **2–1** | Wembley |
| 5 | 1982–83 | Brighton & Hove Albion | **2–2** (a.e.t.), **4–0** (replay) | Wembley (both) |
| 6 | 1984–85 | Everton | **1–0** (a.e.t.) | Wembley |
| 7 | 1989–90 | Crystal Palace | **3–3** (a.e.t.), **1–0** (replay) | Wembley (both) |
| 8 | 1993–94 | Chelsea | **4–0** | Wembley |
| 9 | 1995–96 | Liverpool | **1–0** | Wembley |
| 10 | 1998–99 | Newcastle United | **2–0** | Wembley |
| 11 | 2003–04 | Millwall | **3–0** | Millennium Stadium, Cardiff |
| 12 | 2015–16 | Crystal Palace | **2–1** (a.e.t.) | Wembley |
| 13 | 2023–24 | Manchester City | **2–1** | Wembley |

### Key details

The 1984–85 final saw Norman Whiteside curl in a famous extra-time winner and Kevin Moran become the first player ever sent off in an FA Cup Final. The 1989–90 replay win — **Lee Martin's sole goal** — was Alex Ferguson's first major trophy at United, a result widely credited with saving his job. The 1993–94 rout of Chelsea featured **two Eric Cantona penalties** plus goals from Mark Hughes and Brian McClair. The 1995–96 final was decided by Cantona's iconic late volley, completing a second League and Cup Double. The 1998–99 win over Newcastle (Sheringham, Scholes) was the second leg of the Treble. In 2023–24, academy graduates **Alejandro Garnacho (30') and Kobbie Mainoo (39')** fired United past Manchester City, avenging the previous year's final defeat.

## 4. League Cup / EFL Cup: 6

United's **six League Cup triumphs** place them among the competition's most successful clubs. The trophy has carried various sponsor names across the decades.

### Results

| # | Season | Sponsor name | Opponent | Result |
|---|--------|-------------|----------|--------|
| 1 | 1991–92 | Rumbelows Cup | Nottingham Forest | **1–0** |
| 2 | 2005–06 | Carling Cup | Wigan Athletic | **4–0** |
| 3 | 2008–09 | Carling Cup | Tottenham Hotspur | **0–0** (a.e.t.), **4–1 pens** |
| 4 | 2009–10 | Carling Cup | Aston Villa | **2–1** |
| 5 | 2016–17 | EFL Cup | Southampton | **3–2** |
| 6 | 2022–23 | Carabao Cup | Newcastle United | **2–0** |

### Key details

The 1991–92 win (Brian McClair's goal) was United's first-ever League Cup title. The **2005–06 thrashing of Wigan** featured Wayne Rooney (33', 61'), Louis Saha (55'), and Cristiano Ronaldo (59') at the Millennium Stadium. The 2008–09 final against Spurs was decided by penalties, with **Ben Foster** excelling in goal. United became the first club since Nottingham Forest (1989–90) to retain the League Cup with the 2009–10 win (Michael Owen, Wayne Rooney). The 2016–17 win under José Mourinho saw **Zlatan Ibrahimović score twice** in a 3–2 thriller against Southampton. The 2022–23 triumph under Erik ten Hag (Casemiro, Rashford) ended a six-year trophy drought.

## 5. UEFA Europa League: 1

| Season | Opponent | Result | Venue |
|--------|----------|--------|-------|
| 2016–17 | Ajax | **2–0** | Friends Arena, Stockholm |

On 24 May 2017, goals from **Paul Pogba (18')** — via a deflection off Davinson Sánchez — and **Henrikh Mkhitaryan (48')** earned United a comprehensive win under José Mourinho. The victory made United one of only five clubs at the time to have won all three major UEFA club competitions (Champions League, Europa League, Cup Winners' Cup). The final was played in the shadow of the Manchester Arena bombing two days earlier; captain Wayne Rooney came on as a late substitute in what proved to be among his final appearances.

## 6. UEFA Cup Winners' Cup: 1

| Season | Opponent | Result | Venue |
|--------|----------|--------|-------|
| 1990–91 | Barcelona | **2–1** | De Kuip, Rotterdam |

**Mark Hughes**, a former Barcelona player, scored both goals (67', 74') against his old club. Ronald Koeman pulled one back for Barça. This was the **first European trophy won by an English club** after the post-Heysel five-year ban was lifted, and United's first continental silverware since 1968.

## 7. UEFA Super Cup: 1

| Season | Opponent | Result | Venue |
|--------|----------|--------|-------|
| 1991–92 | Red Star Belgrade | **1–0** | Old Trafford, Manchester |

Scheduled as a two-legged affair, the tie was reduced to a single leg at Old Trafford due to the Yugoslav civil war. **Brian McClair (67')** scored the only goal. United have since lost the Super Cup in 1999 (to Lazio), 2008 (to Zenit St Petersburg), and 2017 (to Real Madrid).

## 8. Intercontinental Cup: 1

| Season | Opponent | Result | Venue |
|--------|----------|--------|-------|

**Roy Keane (35')** capitalised on a goalkeeping error to score the only goal. United remain the only British club to have won the Intercontinental Cup. FIFA retrospectively recognised this as a world club championship in 2017.

## Grand total and how to count "major" trophies

The definition of "major trophy" varies by source. Here is a transparent breakdown:

**Core domestic and European honours (widely accepted as "major"):**

| Competition | Count |
|------------|-------|
| League titles (First Division + Premier League) | 20 |
| FA Cup | 13 |
| League Cup / EFL Cup | 6 |
| European Cup / Champions League | 3 |
| UEFA Europa League | 1 |
| UEFA Cup Winners' Cup | 1 |
| **Subtotal** | **44** |

**World and supranational titles (often included):**

| Competition | Count |
|------------|-------|
| Intercontinental Cup | 1 |
| FIFA Club World Cup | 1 |
| UEFA Super Cup | 1 |
| **Subtotal** | **3** |

**Grand total of major honours: 47** (using the broadest accepted definition, excluding the Community Shield). The Community Shield, classified as a **minor honour**, adds a further **21 titles** for a combined total of **68 trophies** across all competitions.

For production RAG purposes, the safest canonical figure is **44 "core" major trophies** (domestic league + cups + main UEFA competitions). Including world and Super Cup titles brings the count to **47**. The Community Shield should always be listed separately with a clear label as a pre-season/curtain-raiser honour rather than a competitive trophy.
