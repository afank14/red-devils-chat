# Every permanent Manchester United manager: Mangnall to Amorim

**Manchester United have appointed 23 permanent or substantive managers since Ernest Mangnall took charge in 1903**, with only two — Sir Matt Busby and Sir Alex Ferguson — accounting for the vast majority of the club's 68 major trophies. The club's post-Ferguson era has seen eight managers (including caretakers and interims) cycle through Old Trafford in just 13 years, none lasting longer than three seasons. As of March 2026, Michael Carrick serves as interim head coach following Ruben Amorim's dismissal on 5 January 2026.

### Document scope

This document covers every permanent manager in chronological order, including exact tenure dates, full match records, trophies, key signings, tactical philosophies, and legacies. Caretaker-only appointments (Ryan Giggs, Michael Carrick) and interim managers (Ralf Rangnick) are noted but given abbreviated coverage per the user's request. Statistics are drawn from mufcinfo.com, the Wikipedia list of Manchester United managers, and cross-referenced with club and league sources.

## 1. Ernest Mangnall — the man who made United matter

**Full name:** James Ernest Mangnall
**Born:** 4 January 1866, Bolton, Lancashire
**Died:** 13 January 1932
**Status:** Permanent (Secretary-Manager)
**Tenure:** October 1903 – September 1912 (mufcinfo dates: 02/10/1903 – 21/08/1912)

### Match record

| P | W | D | L | Win% |
|---|---|---|---|------|
| 370 | 200 | 76 | 94 | 54.05% |

### Major trophies (5)
- **First Division:** 1907–08, 1910–11
- **FA Cup:** 1908–09
- **FA Charity Shield:** 1908, 1911

### Key signings and tactical approach
Mangnall's masterstroke was raiding Manchester City after their 1906 FA suspension for financial irregularities, signing **Billy Meredith**, **Sandy Turnbull**, **Herbert Burgess**, and **Jimmy Bannister**. He also recruited **Charlie Roberts** (captain and centre-half) and built a team around the legendary half-back line of **Duckworth–Roberts–Bell**. His approach emphasised defensive solidity paired with exciting wide play from Meredith and George Wall, who supplied a mobile forward line.

### Legacy
Mangnall was the **first man to bring major silverware to Manchester United**, winning the club's inaugural league title in 1907–08 by a nine-point margin. He was instrumental in the move from Bank Street, Clayton to **Old Trafford in 1910**. He also founded the Central League and the Football Managers' Association.

### Departure to Manchester City
Mangnall controversially departed directly to rivals Manchester City in September 1912, managing his final United match in a derby (a 1–0 loss) while already planning his move. He remains the only person to have managed both Manchester clubs.

## 2. John "Jack" Robson — United's first official manager

**Full name:** John Robson
**Born:** 24 May 1860, Durham, England
**Died:** 11 January 1922, Manchester
**Status:** Permanent — first person at United to hold the title "manager" rather than "secretary"
**Tenure:** 28 December 1914 – 31 October 1921

*Note: Between Mangnall's departure (1912) and Robson's arrival (1914), the club was run by T.J. Wallworth (acting secretary, Sept–Oct 1912) and then John J. Bentley (secretary, Oct 1912 – Dec 1914). Neither is typically counted as a permanent manager.*

### Match record

| P | W | D | L | Win% |
|---|---|---|---|------|
| 121 | 38 | 36 | 47 | 31.40% |

*Note: Some sources swap the W/D figures (36W/38D). The total of 121 games and 47 losses is consistent across sources.*

### Major trophies
None.

### Key signings and tactical approach
Robson's tenure was devastated by **World War I** — the Football League was suspended from 1915 to 1919. Before the war, the squad was embroiled in the **1915 match-fixing scandal** (United beat Liverpool 2–0 in a fixed match to avoid relegation). Robson had only approximately three full competitive seasons (1919–22) and struggled to rebuild a post-war squad. He had previously managed Middlesbrough, Crystal Palace, and Brighton.

### Legacy
Robson's historic significance lies in establishing the concept of "manager" rather than "secretary" at Manchester United. He stepped down in October 1921 due to severe pneumonia and died just months later on 11 January 1922.

## 3. John Chapman — the mysterious suspension

**Full name:** John Albert Chapman
**Born:** Scotland (exact date undocumented)
**Status:** Permanent (Secretary-Manager)
**Tenure:** 31 October 1921 – 8 October 1926

### Match record

| P | W | D | L | Win% |
|---|---|---|---|------|
| 220 | 86 | 58 | 76 | 39.09% |

### Major trophies
None (though he won promotion via the **Second Division in 1924–25**).

### Key signings and tactical approach
Chapman previously managed Airdrieonians in Scotland. In his first season, United were **relegated from the First Division** for the first time since 1906. He spent three years in the Second Division before winning promotion in 1925 and guiding the club to 9th place and an FA Cup semi-final in 1925–26.

### Legacy
Chapman's tenure ended in one of United's great historical mysteries. On **8 October 1926**, the FA sent a telegram suspending him from all football management for the remainder of the season. No explanation was ever given, and United immediately severed ties. The reason remains unknown to this day.

|---|---|---|---|------|

### Major trophies
None.

### Key details
Hilditch was the **first player-manager in Manchester United's history**, appointed at age 32 as an emergency stand-in. ## 5. Herbert Bamlett — the darkest era

**Full name:** Herbert Bamlett
**Born:** 1 March 1882, England
**Died:** October 1941
**Status:** Permanent
**Tenure:** 13 April 1927 – 9 November 1931

### Match record

| P | W | D | L | Win% |
|---|---|---|---|------|
| 200 | 61 | 48 | 91 | 30.50% |

### Major trophies
None.

### Key details and tactical approach
Bamlett was a former **referee** who officiated the 1914 FA Cup Final before entering management at Oldham Athletic, Wigan Borough, and Middlesbrough. His tenure at United coincided with the death of club owner **John Henry Davies** (October 1927), which triggered severe financial difficulties. In his final season (1930–31), United lost **27 of 42 league matches** and conceded **115 goals** — the worst defensive record in club history. The team suffered a record **12 consecutive league defeats** in 1930 and were relegated to the Second Division.

### Legacy
Widely regarded as one of the least successful managers in United's history, Bamlett oversaw the club's descent into financial ruin and sporting mediocrity. His highest league finish was just 12th in the First Division.

**Full name:** Walter Raymond Crickmer
**Born:** 17 December 1899, Wigan, Lancashire
**Died:** 6 February 1958 (Munich air disaster)
**Status:** Caretaker/Acting Manager (Secretary-Manager in both spells — never held the permanent manager title)
**First spell:** 9 November 1931 – 1 August 1932
**Second spell:** 9 November 1937 – 15 February 1945

### Match record (combined both spells)

| P | W | D | L | Win% |
|---|---|---|---|------|
| 105 | 44 | 28 | 33 | 41.90% |

### Major trophies
None (though he secured **promotion in 1937–38** as Second Division runners-up).

### Key details
Crickmer was not a permanent appointment — he was the club secretary who stepped in when permanent managers departed. He served the club for **39 years** (1919–1958) and was known as "Mr Manchester United." His most critical contribution was **persuading businessman James W. Gibson** to invest in the club in December 1931, saving United from bankruptcy during the Great Depression.

### Crickmer's lasting contributions
Crickmer co-founded United's youth development system, discovered **Johnny Carey**, and helped the club survive the **German bombing of Old Trafford** in 1941. He was tragically killed in the **Munich air disaster** on 6 February 1958.

## 7. Scott Duncan — rock bottom and recovery

**Full name:** Adam Scott Mathieson Duncan
**Born:** 2 November 1888, Dumbarton, Scotland
**Died:** 3 October 1976
**Status:** Permanent (Secretary-Manager)
**Tenure:** 1 August 1932 – 9 November 1937 (mufcinfo dates)

### Match record

| P | W | D | L | Win% |
|---|---|---|---|------|
| 235 | 92 | 53 | 90 | 39.15% |

### Major trophies
- **Second Division Championship:** 1935–36

### Key signings and tactical approach
Duncan inherited a struggling Second Division side with a £30,000 deficit. In his second season (1933–34), he led the club to their **all-time lowest league position: 20th in the Second Division**, avoiding relegation to the Third Division only by beating Millwall 2–0 on the final day. He turned things around, winning the **Second Division title in 1935–36**, but United were immediately relegated again in 1936–37. Together with Walter Crickmer, he was a **co-founder of United's youth development policy**. He notably attempted to sign **Matt Busby** from Manchester City for £250, but the bid was rejected.

### Legacy
Duncan resigned in November 1937 to manage Ipswich Town, where he spent 18 years and mentored a young **Alf Ramsey** (future England World Cup-winning manager). Despite holding the record for United's lowest-ever league finish, he established the youth philosophy that would later produce the Busby Babes.

## 8. Sir Matt Busby — grandfather of modern United

**Full name:** Sir Alexander Matthew Busby CBE KCSG
**Born:** 26 May 1909, Orbiston, Bellshill, Scotland
**Died:** 20 January 1994, Manchester
**Status:** Permanent
**First spell:** 15 February 1945 – 11 August 1969
**Second spell:** 29 December 1970 – 8 June 1971 (temporary return after McGuinness was sacked)

### Match record

|---|---|---|---|------|
| ~1,120–1,141 | ~576 | ~261 | ~304 | 50.45% |

**Second spell:**

| P | W | D | L | Win% |
|---|---|---|---|------|
| 21 | 11 | — | — | ~52% |

*Note: Exact first-spell figures vary slightly by source. The ~50.45% win rate across ~1,120+ matches is the most commonly cited figure. Jimmy Murphy served as acting manager from February to June 1958 while Busby recovered from Munich.*

### Major trophies (first spell — 8 major, plus Charity Shields)
- **First Division:** 1951–52, 1955–56, 1956–57, 1964–65, 1966–67 (5 titles)
- **FA Cup:** 1947–48, 1962–63 (2 cups)
- **European Cup:** 1967–68 (beat Benfica 4–1)
- **FA Charity Shield:** 1952, 1956, 1957

No trophies in second spell.

### Key signings
**Early era:** Johnny Carey (inherited), Stan Pearson, Jack Rowley, Jimmy Delaney
**Busby Babes (developed through youth):** Duncan Edwards, Bobby Charlton, Eddie Colman, Roger Byrne, Mark Jones, David Pegg, Billy Whelan, Bill Foulkes
**Post-Munich rebuild:** Denis Law (1962, British record £115,000 from Torino), Pat Crerand (1963, from Celtic), David Herd (1961, from Arsenal), Tommy Taylor (1953, from Barnsley for £29,999), George Best (scouted at 15 in Belfast, debut 1963), Alex Stepney (1966, goalkeeper from Chelsea)

### Tactical and philosophical approach
Busby **revolutionised English football management** by insisting on total control over team selection, transfers, and training — unprecedented in 1945. He pioneered attacking, entertaining football emphasising youth development, pace, and creativity. He defied the FA to enter the European Cup in 1956–57, championing continental competition for English clubs. His management style was deeply personal: he knew every player's family, built trust and loyalty, and partnered with the irreplaceable assistant **Jimmy Murphy**.

### Legacy — rebuilding from tragedy
Busby **rebuilt Manchester United three times**: post-war (1945–52), post-Munich (1958–63), and the European Cup-winning side (1963–68). The **Munich air disaster** (6 February 1958) killed 8 players and nearly killed Busby himself — he twice received last rites. He rebuilt the club from devastation to European triumph in just 10 years.

### Honours and recognition
Busby became the **first English manager to win the European Cup** in 1968. He was knighted that same year. Busby transformed United from a near-bankrupt yo-yo club into one of the world's most iconic sporting institutions. Sir Matt Busby Way runs outside Old Trafford, and a statue stands at the ground in his honour.

## 9. Wilf McGuinness — impossible shoes to fill

**Full name:** Wilfred McGuinness
**Born:** 25 October 1937, Manchester
**Died:** 28 August 2021
**Status:** Permanent (initially appointed as "chief coach," then promoted to manager)
**Tenure:** 11 August 1969 – 27 December 1970

### Match record

| P | W | D | L | Win% |
|---|---|---|---|------|
| 88 | 32 | 33 | 23 | 36.36% |

### Major trophies
None. Reached three cup semi-finals (one FA Cup, two League Cups).

### Background
McGuinness was a **former Busby Babe** whose playing career ended at 22 after a broken leg. At just 31, he became the **youngest manager in First Division history**. He inherited an ageing squad (Charlton, Law, Best).

### Challenges and dismissal
McGuinness was fatally undermined by **Busby's continued presence** as general manager — senior players reportedly bypassed McGuinness to consult Busby directly. He finished 8th in 1969–70 (actually an improvement on Busby's final full season of 11th) but was sacked on 27 December 1970. His failure illustrated the enormous difficulty of succeeding a legendary manager — a pattern that would repeat after Ferguson retired in 2013.

## 10. Frank O'Farrell — dignity amid dysfunction

**Full name:** Francis O'Farrell
**Born:** 9 October 1927, Cork, Ireland
**Died:** 6 March 2022, Torquay
**Status:** Permanent (five-year contract)
**Tenure:** 8 June 1971 – 19 December 1972

### Match record

| P | W | D | L | Win% |
|---|---|---|---|------|
| 81 | 30 | 24 | 27 | 37.04% |

### Major trophies
None. League finishes: 8th (1971–72), 18th at time of sacking.

### Key signings
- **Martin Buchan** (from Aberdeen, £125,000) — became a long-serving captain
- **Ian Storey-Moore** (from Nottingham Forest, £200,000) — injury-plagued
- **Ted MacDougall** (from Bournemouth, £200,000) — flopped, sold within 6 months

### Tactical approach
O'Farrell was a product of the **West Ham coaching academy** and favoured organised, methodical football. His first season began brilliantly — United lost just 1 of their first 14 league matches and briefly topped the table. But seven consecutive league defeats after New Year's Day 1972 began a dramatic collapse.

### Legacy
The fundamental problem was **George Best's deteriorating off-field behaviour**, which O'Farrell couldn't control. Like McGuinness, he was undermined by Busby's continuing boardroom influence. He was sacked on 19 December 1972, the same day Best was transfer-listed, after a humiliating **5–0 loss to Crystal Palace**. O'Farrell remains the **only Irish manager in United's history**.

## 11. Tommy Docherty — relegation, redemption, and scandal

**Full name:** Thomas Henderson Docherty
**Born:** 24 April 1928, Glasgow
**Died:** 31 December 2020
**Status:** Permanent
**Tenure:** 22 December 1972 – 4 July 1977

### Match record

| P | W | D | L | Win% |
|---|---|---|---|------|
| 228 | 107 | 56 | 65 | 46.93% |

### Major trophies (2)
- **Second Division Championship:** 1974–75
- **FA Cup:** 1976–77 (beat Liverpool 2–1)

### Key signings
**Lou Macari** (from Celtic, £200,000), **Stuart Pearson** (from Hull City), **Steve Coppell** (from Tranmere Rovers), **Gordon Hill** (from Millwall), **Jimmy Greenhoff** (from Stoke City)

### Tactical approach
Docherty played an attacking, entertaining **4-2-4 formation** with emphasis on width through wingers Coppell and Hill. He described his defensive pressing as "flies around a sugar bowl" and his philosophy aligned with the Busby tradition of pleasing and exciting fans. He cleared out the aging Busby-era stars and assembled an exciting, youthful side.

### Legacy
Docherty could not prevent **relegation in 1973–74** (United's first since 1937) but won immediate promotion as Second Division champions. The **1977 FA Cup triumph** over Liverpool — who had just won the league and were about to win the European Cup — remains one of United's most celebrated finals. He was sacked just weeks later after his extramarital affair with physiotherapist Laurie Brown's wife became public. His colourful personality and sharp wit ("I've always said there's a place for the press, but they haven't dug it yet") made him a beloved figure.

## 12. Dave Sexton — tactical innovator, crowd divider

**Full name:** David James Sexton OBE
**Born:** 6 April 1930, Islington, London
**Died:** 25 November 2012
**Status:** Permanent
**Tenure:** 14 July 1977 – 30 April 1981

### Match record

| P | W | D | L | Win% |
|---|---|---|---|------|
| 201 | 81 | 64 | 56 | 40.30% |

### Major trophies
None.

### Key signings
**Gordon McQueen** and **Joe Jordan** (both from Leeds United, 1978), **Ray Wilkins** (from Chelsea, 1979), **Gary Bailey** (promoted from trial), **Garry Birtles** (from Nottingham Forest, club record £1.25m — a notorious flop)

### Tactical approach
Sexton was the polar opposite of Docherty: **defensive, disciplined, and tactically cerebral**. A coaching innovator who used match-analysis footage, he emphasised fitness, structure, and counter-attacking play. His approach was criticised as "dour" by fans who craved the excitement of the Docherty era.

### Legacy
Despite solid results — including a league **runners-up finish in 1979–80** (two points behind Liverpool) and a dramatic **1979 FA Cup final** (lost 3–2 to Arsenal after leading 2–0 with five minutes remaining) — Sexton was dismissed on 30 April 1981 despite **winning his final seven consecutive games**. The board deemed four seasons without a trophy unacceptable. He later led England U-21s to two European Championship titles (1982, 1984) and was awarded an OBE.

## 13. Ron Atkinson — champagne football without the league title

**Full name:** Ronald Frederick Atkinson
**Born:** 18 March 1939, Liverpool
**Status:** Permanent
**Tenure:** 9 June 1981 – 6 November 1986 (mufcinfo: 04/11/1986)

### Match record

| P | W | D | L | Win% |
|---|---|---|---|------|
| 292 | 146 | 79 | 67 | 50.00% |

### Major trophies (3)
- **FA Cup:** 1982–83 (beat Brighton 4–0 in replay), 1984–85 (beat Everton 1–0)
- **FA Charity Shield:** 1983

### Key signings
**Bryan Robson** (from West Brom, British record £1.5m — became United's inspirational captain for over a decade), **Frank Stapleton** (from Arsenal), **Arnold Mühren** (from Ipswich), **Gordon Strachan** (from Aberdeen), **Jesper Olsen** (from Ajax). Youth products **Norman Whiteside**, **Mark Hughes**, and **Paul McGrath** all emerged under Atkinson.

### Tactical approach
Atkinson advocated **"champagne football"** — expansive, attacking, and entertaining. He continued the Busby/Docherty tradition and invested in quality flair players. United under Atkinson were **dominant in cup competitions** but consistently failed to sustain league title challenges, never finishing below 4th in his first five full seasons but never finishing higher than 3rd.

### Legacy
The **1985–86 season** epitomised Atkinson's tenure: United won their first **10 consecutive league games** (a club record), then faded to 4th. The following season began disastrously — after a humiliating **4–1 League Cup defeat to Southampton** on 5 November 1986, with United near the bottom, Atkinson was sacked. His most enduring legacy was signing Bryan Robson and developing youth talent. He stated: "I left United with the best record, at that time, since the great Sir Matt Busby."

## 14. Sir Alex Ferguson — the greatest manager in football history

**Full name:** Sir Alexander Chapman Ferguson CBE
**Born:** 31 December 1941, Govan, Glasgow
**Status:** Permanent
**Tenure:** 6 November 1986 – 19 May 2013 (26 years, 194 days)

### Match record

| **1,500** | **895** | **338** | **267** | **59.67%** |

**Premier League only:** 810 P, 528 W, 168 D, 114 L (**65.2% win rate**)

### Complete trophy list — 38 trophies

**Premier League / First Division (13):** 1992–93, 1993–94, 1995–96, 1996–97, 1998–99, 1999–00, 2000–01, 2002–03, 2006–07, 2007–08, 2008–09, 2010–11, 2012–13

**FA Cup (5):** 1989–90, 1993–94, 1995–96, 1998–99, 2003–04

**League Cup (4):** 1991–92, 2005–06, 2008–09, 2009–10

**UEFA Champions League (2):** 1998–99, 2007–08

**UEFA Super Cup (1):** 1991

**Intercontinental Cup (1):** 1999

**FIFA Club World Cup (1):** 2008

**FA Charity Shield / Community Shield (10):** 1990 (shared), 1993, 1994, 1996, 1997, 2003, 2007, 2008, 2010, 2011

### Key signings — foundation years (1986–92)
Ferguson signed Steve Bruce, Gary Pallister, Paul Ince, Mark Hughes (return), Denis Irwin, **Peter Schmeichel** (£505k — arguably the Premier League's greatest goalkeeper bargain), and Andrei Kanchelskis.

### Key signings — first dynasty (1992–97)
Ferguson signed **Eric Cantona** (£1.2m from Leeds — transformative catalyst for ending 26-year title drought), **Roy Keane** (£3.75m British record from Nottingham Forest), and Andy Cole (£7m British record from Newcastle). The **Class of '92** emerged: Ryan Giggs, David Beckham, Paul Scholes, Gary Neville, Phil Neville, Nicky Butt.

### Key signings — Treble and dominance (1997–2003)
Ferguson added Teddy Sheringham, Jaap Stam, Dwight Yorke, **Ole Gunnar Solskjær** (£1.5m), Ruud van Nistelrooy, and **Rio Ferdinand** (£29.1m British record).

### Key signings — Ronaldo era (2003–09)
Ferguson signed **Cristiano Ronaldo** (£12.24m from Sporting — evolved into world's best player, Ballon d'Or 2008), **Wayne Rooney** (£25.6m from Everton — became United's all-time top scorer with 253 goals), Nemanja Vidic, Patrice Evra, Michael Carrick, and Edwin van der Sar.

### Key signings — final years (2009–13)
Ferguson's late additions included David de Gea, Javier Hernandez, and **Robin van Persie** (2012, from Arsenal — 26 goals to clinch Ferguson's final title).

### Tactical evolution
Ferguson's tactical philosophy was built on **pace, width, penetration, and unpredictability**, with traditional wingers as a hallmark across all eras. His approach evolved from a classic **4-4-2** (1986–1999) to more fluid **4-3-3 and 4-2-3-1** formations in the Ronaldo era (2006–09) and final years. Constants included commitment to youth development, a "never-say-die" mentality (the phenomenon of **"Fergie Time"** — United's extraordinary ability to score late winners), and supreme man-management combining the famous **"hairdryer treatment"** with emotional intelligence tailored to each player.

### Records
Ferguson is the **most decorated manager in football history** with 49 career trophies (38 at United). He won **13 Premier League titles** — nine more than any other manager. He is the **only manager to win three consecutive English top-flight titles twice** (1999–2001, 2007–2009). His **528 Premier League wins** remain the all-time record. He won **27 Manager of the Month awards** and **11 Manager of the Season awards**.

### The Treble and lasting legacy
Ferguson was knighted in 1999 after the unprecedented **Treble** (Premier League, FA Cup, Champions League), with Sheringham and Solskjaer scoring in injury time to beat Bayern Munich 2–1 in the Champions League final. Ferguson's 2010–11 league title was United's **19th, overtaking Liverpool's record of 18** — a stated objective from the start of his reign.

His 2017–18 saw United finish **2nd** in the Premier League — the best league finish since Ferguson — but 19 points behind Manchester City.

### Legacy
Mourinho was sacked on 18 December 2018 after a **3–1 loss at Liverpool** with United 11th in the table, 19 points off the leaders. His "third-season syndrome" repeated from previous clubs. His increasingly toxic relationship with **Paul Pogba** (stripped of vice-captaincy) and public criticism of players like Luke Shaw undermined the dressing room. Still, he remains the only post-Ferguson manager to win a European trophy.

**Full name:** Rúben Filipe Marques Amorim
**Born:** 27 January 1985, Lisbon, Portugal

None. Reached the **2024–25 Europa League final** (lost to Tottenham).

### Key signings
The club spent approximately **£250m** during Amorim's tenure across the January 2025 and summer 2025 windows. Notable arrivals included **Benjamin Šeško**, **Bryan Mbeumo**, and **Matheus Cunha** (summer 2025).

### Tactical approach
Amorim's non-negotiable **3-4-3 formation** — his system from Sporting CP — proved deeply problematic. He started **45 of 47 Premier League matches with three at the back** and famously declared "not even the Pope" could make him change his system (September 2025). He briefly abandoned the back three for a 1–0 win over Newcastle on Boxing Day 2025 but quickly reverted.

### Results overview
Amorim's first season produced **15th place** — United's worst finish since relegation in 1974. The Europa League final run (including a dramatic semi-final comeback against Lyon) was a bright spot, but the league record was catastrophic. In 2025–26, results marginally improved (31 points from 20 matches), and he coached United's **first away win at Anfield since 2016** (2–1, October 2025).

### Dismissal
A **shock League Cup exit to Grimsby Town** (League Two) and heavy league defeats defined Amorim's reign. His public statement after a 1–1 draw at Leeds on 4 January 2026 — "I am the manager, not the coach" — and a swipe at director of football Jason Wilcox proved his undoing. He was **sacked the following day**, 5 January 2026. Sky Sports reported his "emotional and inconsistent behaviour" was a key factor. He departed with United in 6th place, three points off the top four.

## Conclusion: a club defined by two towering figures

Manchester United's 123-year managerial history reveals a stark truth: virtually all sustained success traces back to just two men. **Sir Matt Busby** (8 major trophies, 1945–69) and **Sir Alex Ferguson** (28 major trophies, 1986–2013) account for **36 of the club's approximately 43 major honours** (excluding Charity/Community Shields). The periods between them — from 1969 to 1986 — produced just **three FA Cups** across five managers. The post-Ferguson era, now spanning 13 years and eight managers (plus caretakers), has yielded **four major trophies** (FA Cup 2016, Europa League 2017, League Cup 2017, League Cup 2023, FA Cup 2024 — five total), with no manager lasting more than three years and no league title since 2013.

### The recurring pattern of instability

The pattern of managerial instability after a long-serving legend is strikingly consistent: both Busby and Ferguson were followed by immediate successors who failed quickly (McGuinness and Moyes, each lasting roughly one season), then by a sequence of shorter-term appointments. The club's March 2026 situation — with Michael Carrick serving as interim while United seek yet another permanent appointment — suggests this cycle remains unbroken. What United's next manager inherits is a club that has spent over **£2 billion on transfers** since Ferguson retired, changed tactical systems repeatedly, and yet remains further from the summit than at any point since the pre-Busby 1930s.
